Saxo Bank had just upgraded its AI chatbot engine. The directive from leadership was clear: make it the default support channel across all platforms. There was a more radical idea on the table too — remove all other help options entirely and go chatbot-only.

Before I touched a single frame, I needed to test that assumption. In a financial context, where a wrong answer or a dead end can mean real money lost, I wasn't going to recommend removing safety nets without evidence.

"Support behaviour was diverse. No single channel dominated. The chatbot-only idea was tested — and the data didn't support it."

I spent the entire first week in research — 12 user interviews, a UX audit across both platforms, and a competitive sweep of Intercom and Binance. All three streams pointed to the same place.

UX audit — Saxo Investor desktop vs TraderGO modal

The problem wasn't the chatbot.
It was access.

Investor used a full help page — spacious, but once you left your workflow you were on your own. TraderGO had a persistent modal — always there, but stateless, cramped, and reset every session. Mobile inherited all of TraderGO's problems on a smaller screen.

The fix wasn't to pick one and remove the other. It was to design a unified pattern that worked consistently across all three — and to make sure users could always reach the support they actually needed.

Wireframe explorations — defining the modal pattern

One modal. Three platforms.
Chat-first, but not chat-only.

The solution: a single modal that opens chat-first — putting the new AI chatbot front and centre — but keeps the help centre one tap away via a persistent tab. Resizable, minimisable, with full state persistence. Your conversation stays with you.

Three states were designed and handed off: the default chat view, the help centre with article browsing and search, and an expanded view for reading longer articles without losing context.

One template. All three platforms. Delivered in three weeks.

Interactive prototype — tested with 8 participants in guerrilla sessions

Reflection

What I pushed back on — and what I'd do differently

The most important design decision on this project happened before I opened Figma: resisting the chatbot-only brief. Research gave me the evidence to push back, and the team was receptive. That conversation early on shaped everything that followed — and the results validated it.

What I'd do differently: build in a 60-day research checkpoint post-launch. We knew users understood the new interface and were willing to try the chatbot. We didn't know whether they'd keep choosing it over time.

I'd also push earlier for shared success metrics across design, product, and customer support — we aligned on them late, which made the post-launch story harder to tell internally.
