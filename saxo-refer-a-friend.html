Refer a Friend

Saxo Bank had a referral programme with real potential — but engagement was well below what the business needed. I redesigned it from the ground up and built a globally scalable template that shipped across 14 markets.

Final solution — Refer a Friend across Saxo platforms

Challenge
A referral programme with untapped potential. Users struggled to find it, the reward mechanics were unclear, and there was no feedback loop to keep referrers engaged.
Strategy
Redesign the entire experience end to end — and build a globally scalable template that could hold across 14 markets without fragmenting into 14 versions.
Results
3.2× year-over-year referral growth. 20% increase directly from Referral 2.0. 50% reduction in support questions about the programme.
The brief

The infrastructure was in place. The experience wasn't working hard enough.

Saxo Bank's referral programme had been running quietly in the background — tracking set up, reward mechanics in place, comms going out. But engagement was underwhelming given the size of Saxo's client base. Referral 2.0 was the mandate to change that: give the programme a proper identity, fix the experience end to end, and make it scale globally.

The constraints were real from the start. The design system was locked — no new components. Legal requirements varied dramatically across markets. And the timeline was subject to compliance review cycles that could move in any direction at any time.

The problem

Three friction points. All pointing at the same root cause.

Funnel analysis and usability testing across both the referrer and referred user experience told a consistent story. Users weren't disengaged because they didn't want to refer — the friction in the experience was getting in the way before they had a chance to act.

  • Entry points buried in the profile menu — the referral icon was a speakerphone that didn't signal the value of what was inside
  • Commission credits were opaque — users didn't know what they were, where they appeared, or how to use them
  • No feedback loop — once a referral link was sent, it disappeared into a void
  • Landing pages for referred users felt disconnected from the main platform
  • Legal copy was visually prominent in the flow, pulling attention away from the action we wanted users to take

"I sent the link to a friend but I have no idea if he ever did anything with it."

The strategy

Entry points, done with restraint.

We mapped the most visited pages across Saxo's platforms and identified where referral touchpoints would make sense. The temptation — and the pressure from leadership — was to be aggressive. Put it everywhere. We pushed back. Plastering referral CTAs across every surface would create noise and erode the trust we were trying to build.

What we shipped: the speakerphone replaced with a gift box icon on both mobile and desktop across both platforms, a button on the portfolio page, and a list item in the profile card. Focused, intentional, non-intrusive. Leadership was skeptical. The results proved the call was right.

Making the reward legible.

The commission credits hub was the centrepiece of the redesign — a dedicated section that explained what credits were, how they were earned, where they appeared in the platform, and how to use them. This alone resolved the core confusion that testing had surfaced repeatedly.

The persistent funding reminder closed the broken feedback loop. Referrers could now see the status of their referral until the 90-day window closed or their contact funded an account. The void was gone.

One template. Not fourteen.

The bigger design challenge was building something that could work everywhere without becoming everything. Saxo has offices across Europe, Asia, the Middle East and beyond — each with different legal frameworks, different cultural contexts, and different ideas about what the referral programme should look like.

Denmark, Holland and Belgium operate under strict pyramid scheme laws. The referrer cannot receive anything for referring someone. We made the call to rename the programme "Invite a Friend" in those markets and restructure the reward to apply only to the referred user. Legal didn't have bandwidth to rule on every decision — we made it transparent and moved forward.

Singapore was the opposite — multiple reward types for different circumstances, a far more permissive environment. A challenge that emerged mid-design phase was accommodating this reward diversity structurally. Europe was limited to commission credits, while other markets were open to cash prizes as well. The design had to scale to both without requiring separate templates — something I resolved by building the reward component to support either format within the same layout.

Navigating stakeholder pressure

Maintaining the global template wasn't always straightforward. Local offices had strong opinions about how their market should be represented, and there were conversations — including with senior leadership — where the design rationale needed to be explained and defended. The approach held, and the results supported it.

Cultural adaptation at scale.

Holding one template didn't mean ignoring culture. Two Nordic-looking people on a referral banner is not a fit for Japan, Singapore or Dubai. I built individual asset stacks for each market — and the technical constraints on banner sizes made this genuinely difficult. By the end I'd written a new composition guideline for all future banner updates across the programme.

Closing the loop — the web referral journey.

The referral experience extended beyond the platform. When a referred user clicks a referral link, they land on a public-facing page on home.saxo before they've ever logged in. That first impression is critical — and it was one of the biggest drop-off points in the original funnel.

Working closely with the web design team, I provided UX direction and a design foundation they could build from. The page needed to feel trustworthy, clear about the reward, and consistent with the platform experience the referred user would eventually sign up for. We worked through several rounds of feedback until the hierarchy and messaging felt right.

That page is currently converting 1 in 5 referred visitors into new Saxo clients — outperforming every other acquisition feature on the platform across all markets.

What didn't ship

The features that got to the finish line — and the ones that didn't.

The invitee list was the feature I believed in most. Not a leaderboard — a simple view of who you'd invited and where they were in the journey. Nudge design. Social proof. A closed loop. Compliance approved it, then retracted their approval weeks before launch. We absorbed it and shipped without it.

The modular content slot for local offices was fully built and is sitting in the design documentation and code — available if the business ever decides to activate it.

Results

3.2× growth. The programme realised its potential.

Within a year of launch, referral volume was 3.2 times larger year-over-year. Referral 2.0 directly drove a 20% increase. Support questions about the programme dropped by 50% — users were no longer confused about how it worked.

A concurrent marketing campaign around Saxo's new pricing may have contributed to broader word-of-mouth referrals. The metrics are tracked directly to the referral flow — but I'd rather be honest about the context than overstate the attribution.

Reflection

What I'd do differently — and what I'd fight harder for.

I'd build compliance into discovery next time, not treat it as a final gate. Losing the invitee list weeks before launch — after it had been approved — was avoidable with earlier, deeper alignment. It was the feature I believed in most and it never shipped.

Taking full ownership of UX and UI through to dev handoff wasn't the plan. Both UI designers on the project were unavailable by the time I reached high-fidelity. The handoff worked — the programme shipped and the results followed. But the UI would have been stronger with dedicated craft time, and I'd fight for that resource earlier next time rather than absorb the gap at the end.

The tracking framework I set up early paid off. I mapped out every tracking point across the flow and worked with the data team to define what was technically measurable before a single screen went to dev. Next time I'd do it even earlier — and tie it to shared success metrics across design, product and data from day one.
