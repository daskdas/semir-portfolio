The brief

A programme that existed on paper but failed in practice

Saxo Bank had a referral programme, but it wasn't doing much. The design and entry points failed to motivate action — users either didn't know it existed or couldn't figure out how the rewards worked. The business goal was clear: revitalise the programme to increase engagement, referral completions, and funded accounts.

Added complexity came from regulatory constraints. Denmark's Pyramid Scheme Act placed specific limits on how referral rewards could be structured and communicated, and cross-market variations (Denmark vs Dubai, among others) required careful legal alignment throughout.

Research

Two parallel tracks — funnel data and usability testing — running simultaneously.

Discovery combined quantitative drop-off analysis with qualitative speak-aloud testing to understand both where users were failing and why.

01 Funnel analysis — mapping the drop-off

Working with data analysts and PMs, I mapped the referral funnel end to end. The clearest signal: users were generating referral links, but referred users weren't completing sign-up or funding their accounts. Drop-off happened at two points — referred users abandoning the landing page, and referrers losing track of the process.

What the data showed

The problem wasn't awareness of the reward — it was trust and clarity. Referred users arrived on landing pages that felt inconsistent with the main platform, and referrers had no way to track whether their referral had done anything.

02 Usability testing — 7 speak-aloud sessions, both sides of the loop

I ran 7 speak-aloud usability tests covering both the referrer and the referred user. Testing old and new concepts in parallel helped isolate what was genuinely broken versus what could be improved.

Key issues surfaced:

  • Confusion about how "commission credits" are earned, where they appear, and how to spend them
  • Entry points in the profile menu and portfolio banner were largely ignored — users scrolled past them entirely
  • Landing pages for referred users felt inconsistent and untrustworthy
  • A large terms & conditions CTA dominated the visual hierarchy, overshadowing the actual action
  • No feedback or progress tracking — referrers had no idea if their contact had signed up or funded
The key insight

Users weren't confused about what a referral programme was. They were confused about what would happen next — and that uncertainty was enough to stop them from acting.

Research summary

Both streams pointed to the same root cause: visibility, clarity, and feedback. Users needed to find the feature, understand the reward, and know where they stood in the process at all times.
