Refer a Friend — Revitalising a Dormant Growth Channel

Saxo Bank had a referral programme that technically existed but barely worked. Low visibility, unclear rewards, and a confusing flow meant users weren't engaging. I redesigned it end-to-end — and tripled referred users within a year.

The brief

A programme that existed on paper but failed in practice

Saxo Bank had a referral programme, but it wasn't doing much. The design and entry points failed to motivate action — users either didn't know it existed or couldn't figure out how the rewards worked. The business goal was clear: revitalise the programme to increase engagement, referral completions, and funded accounts.

Added complexity came from regulatory constraints. Denmark's Pyramid Scheme Act placed specific limits on how referral rewards could be structured and communicated, and cross-market variations (Denmark vs Dubai, among others) required careful legal alignment throughout.

Research

The problem wasn't motivation — it was visibility and clarity

Before touching any designs, I ran a full discovery phase to understand where and why the funnel was failing.

01 — Funnel analysis

Drop-off happened after link generation — before funding

Working with data analysts via the PM, I mapped the referral funnel end-to-end. The clearest signal: most users who generated a referral link never saw it convert to a funded account. The drop happened at two points — referred users not completing sign-up, and referred sign-ups not funding their accounts.

Key finding

The funnel was leaking at completion, not at initiation. People were willing to share — they just couldn't get their referrals over the finish line.

02 — Usability testing

7 speak-aloud sessions with both referrers and referred users

I ran structured usability tests with participants on both sides of the flow — the person doing the referring and the person receiving the referral. Testing both sides was critical because the experience breaks in different places depending on which role you're in.

Five consistent pain points emerged:

  • Confusion about "commission credits" — users didn't understand what they were, how they were earned, or where to find them
  • Entry points were invisible — the profile button and portfolio banner were largely ignored
  • The referred landing page felt untrustworthy and inconsistent with the main platform
  • A large terms & conditions CTA overshadowed the main action buttons — completely backwards hierarchy
  • No progress tracking — users had no way to know if their referral was working
The insight that changed everything

Users weren't disinterested in the programme — they were confused and unsupported. Fix the clarity and the visibility, and the motivation was already there.

03 — Competitive analysis

Best-in-class referral programmes use behavioural reinforcement

I reviewed referral mechanics across fintech and beyond — looking at how platforms like Revolut, Trading 212, and others structured their programmes. The pattern was consistent: the most effective referral flows used persistent reminders, clear progress indicators, and multiple sharing surfaces rather than a single buried entry point.

This validated the direction: the solution needed to meet users at multiple points in their journey, not just when they happened to find the referral page.

Process

From wireframes to full ownership

The project started as a collaboration between myself and UI designers, but mid-project I took on full end-to-end ownership — wireframes, flows, and final high-fidelity designs — after other designers left. This meant moving faster and making more decisions independently, while staying closely aligned with the PM, legal, and compliance teams.

The solution

Visibility, clarity, and behavioural reinforcement — at every step

The redesign addressed every failure point identified in research. Rather than a single fix, it was a systemic overhaul of the entire referral experience — from first discovery through to completion.

  • Dedicated page over dialog — replaced the cramped dialog with a full page, giving room for proper explanation of how the programme works
  • Commission credits hub — a clear explanation of what credits are, how they're earned, where to find them, and how to use them — solving the single biggest confusion point
  • Persistent funding reminder — visible until the referred user funds their account or the 90-day window expires, keeping the referrer informed and motivated
  • Improved landing page — trustworthy, consistent with the platform, with clear instructions for referred users on what to do next
  • Reduced friction everywhere — QR code sharing, simplified copy, redesigned trigger emails, and helpful empty states guiding first-time users
  • Cross-platform consistency — aligned experience between web and the trading platform
The paused feature

A leaderboard and referral status dashboard were fully designed and internally validated — they tested well for social reinforcement. They were paused pre-launch due to compliance concerns around competitive framing in regulated markets. The rest shipped.

Final hi-fi — dedicated referral page, commission credits hub, and persistent funding reminder

Results

Tripled referred users. Halved support questions.

Within one year post-launch, the redesigned programme delivered measurable impact across every key metric. The 50% reduction in support questions is particularly telling — it signals that users understood the experience well enough to not need help, which was exactly the goal.

Reflection

Compliance killed the leaderboard. I'd find another way.

The leaderboard and referral dashboard were the features I was most excited about — they tested well and would have added real social reinforcement to the programme. Losing them to compliance concerns late in the process was frustrating, but the lesson was clear: involve legal earlier in the ideation phase, not just the delivery phase.

If I were doing this again, I'd build a milestone-based progress indicator as the primary feedback loop from the start — less regulatory risk than a public leaderboard, but still gives users the sense of momentum that drives completion.

I'd also push for A/B testing on reward framing and copy earlier. We had strong hypotheses about what would motivate different user segments, but didn't have the infrastructure to test them at launch. That's a conversation worth having with product and engineering before the project starts, not after.
